<h2>Doctrines &amp; Overruling Pairs to Memorise</h2>
<p>Beyond individual cases, UPSC tests a compact set of <strong>doctrines</strong> that recur across judgments. Fix these firmly, because a single doctrine can answer many questions:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Basic Structure Doctrine</strong> (Kesavananda Bharati, reaffirmed in Minerva Mills) — the amending power under Art. 368 cannot destroy the Constitution's core.</li>
<li><strong>Substantive equality</strong> (Maneka Gandhi, Davinder Singh) — equality is not merely formal; reasonable classification and affirmative action are permitted.</li>
<li><strong>Creamy layer &amp; the 50% ceiling</strong> (Indra Sawhney) — the backbone of reservation jurisprudence.</li>
<li><strong>Constitutional morality</strong> (Navtej Johar, Puttaswamy) — rights of individuals and minorities cannot be subordinated to majoritarian sentiment.</li>
<li><strong>Due process &amp; natural justice</strong> (Maneka Gandhi, the bulldozer case) — state action affecting life, liberty or property must follow fair procedure.</li>
</ul>
<p>Equally important are the <strong>overruling pairs</strong>, a favourite Prelims trap: <em>E.V. Chinnaiah (2004) → overruled by Davinder Singh (2024)</em> on SC/ST sub-classification; <em>S. Azeez Basha (1967) → overruled by the AMU judgment (2024)</em> on minority status. Knowing which case displaced which is often the difference between a right and wrong answer.</p>

<h2>How to Use Judgments in Prelims</h2>
<p>UPSC Prelims tests judgments in predictable ways: linking a <strong>case to an article or right</strong> ("The Right to Privacy was declared a fundamental right under which Article?"), asking about a <strong>doctrine</strong> (basic structure, creamy layer, 50% cap), or a <strong>statement-based</strong> check on what a judgment held. Your revision should therefore:</p>
<ul>
<li>Memorise the <strong>article-to-case</strong> mapping first (the second column of each table).</li>
<li>Fix the <strong>one-line holding</strong> — what exactly the Court decided.</li>
<li>Note whether a judgment <strong>overruled</strong> an earlier case (Davinder Singh overruled Chinnaiah; AMU overruled Azeez Basha) — a favourite distractor.</li>
<li>Do not attempt to memorise paragraph-level reasoning for Prelims; that is Mains material.</li>
</ul>

<h2>How to Use Judgments in Mains Answers</h2>
<p>This is where judgments become high-value. A well-placed case citation signals depth and lifts an average answer. Use this discipline:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Name + one-line holding.</strong> "In <em>Puttaswamy</em> (2017), the Supreme Court held that privacy is a fundamental right under Article 21" — that is enough; do not narrate the whole case.</li>
<li><strong>Link to the question's theme.</strong> Electoral Bonds → electoral reforms / RTI; S.R. Bommai → federalism / misuse of Art. 356; Davinder Singh → substantive equality; bulldozer case → rule of law and due process.</li>
<li><strong>Use judgments to balance an argument.</strong> Cite one case for a principle and, where relevant, note a limitation or a subsequent development (as with the Governor's-assent timelines being revisited in 2025).</li>
<li><strong>Avoid legal overclaiming.</strong> You are an administrator-in-training: state the holding accurately, do not invent ratios, and do not editorialise about "right" or "wrong" verdicts.</li>
<li><strong>Build a small, reusable bank</strong> of 15-20 judgments mapped to GS-II themes (rights, federalism, separation of powers, social justice) and reuse them.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Common Mistakes with Judgments</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Confusing which case did what</strong> — especially overruling pairs (Chinnaiah/Davinder Singh; Azeez Basha/AMU).</li>
<li><strong>Over-narrating in Mains</strong> instead of a crisp name + holding + link.</li>
<li><strong>Legal overclaiming</strong> — inventing ratios or misstating the holding.</li>
<li><strong>Ignoring subsequent developments</strong> — treating the April 2025 Governor timelines as settled when a 2025 Presidential Reference revisited them.</li>
<li><strong>Skipping the landmarks</strong> — the static classics are tested every single year.</li>
<li><strong>Relying on memory</strong> instead of verifying the holding on an authoritative source.</li>
</ol>

- In Re Article 370 — 11 Dec 2023, upheld abrogation, temporary provision. Source: official SC PDF (api.sci.gov.in). ✔
- Davinder Singh — 2024 INSC 562, 1 Aug 2024, 6:1, sub-classification permitted, overruled E.V. Chinnaiah. Source: SC Observer; Wikipedia. ✔
- Property Owners Assn v. Maharashtra — 5 Nov 2024, 9-judge, majority (reported 7:2 by SCC Online), not all private property = "material resources of community"; Art 31C survives. Source: official SC PDF (api.sci.gov.in); SCC Online. Split stated as "majority, with dissent" to avoid overclaiming the exact ratio. ✔
- Section 6A Citizenship — 17 Oct 2024, 4:1, upheld; cut-off 25 Mar 1971. Source: official SC PDF (api.sci.gov.in); The Hindu; Indian Express. ✔
- Bulldozer — In Re: Directions in the Matter of Demolition of Structures, 2024 INSC 866, 13 Nov 2024, Art 142 guidelines, due process. Source: SC Observer; The Hindu. ✔
- AMU v. Naresh Agarwal — 8 Nov 2024, 7-judge 4:3, overruled Azeez Basha (1967), test laid down, AMU-specific question remanded. Source: SC Observer; The Hindu; Indian Express. ✔
