<BODY>
<p><strong>What happens after clearing UPSC?</strong> The final result is the beginning, not the end. Once the Union Public Service Commission publishes the merit list, the <strong>Department of Personnel and Training (DoPT)</strong> takes over: you go through <strong>service allocation</strong> (based on your rank, category and preferences), a <strong>medical examination</strong>, document and background verification, and — for All India Service recruits — <strong>cadre allocation</strong> to a state. You then begin your journey as an <strong>officer trainee</strong> with the common <strong>Foundation Course at LBSNAA, Mussoorie</strong>, before moving to your service-specific academy — IAS stays at LBSNAA, IPS goes to SVPNPA Hyderabad, IFS to SSIFS Delhi, and IRS to NADT Nagpur or NACIN. After professional and district training, you take up your <strong>first posting</strong> as an officer.</p>
<p>This guide walks through every stage in order, using official sources, so you understand exactly what life looks like on the other side of the result. If you are still building your foundation, see our <a href="/resources/upsc-beginners-guide">UPSC beginner's guide</a> and the <a href="/resources/upsc-exam-pattern-syllabus">exam pattern &amp; syllabus explainer</a>.</p>

<h2>Key Takeaways</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>DoPT runs the post-result process:</strong> service allocation → medical &amp; verification → cadre allocation → training.</li>
<li><strong>Service allocation</strong> depends on rank, category and DAF-II preferences against that year's vacancies.</li>
<li><strong>All officers start together</strong> at the ~15-week common <strong>Foundation Course at LBSNAA</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>Then services split:</strong> IAS → LBSNAA; IPS → SVPNPA Hyderabad; IFS → SSIFS Delhi; IRS-IT → NADT Nagpur; IRS-C&amp;IT → NACIN.</li>
<li><strong>Cadre allocation</strong> (for IAS/IPS/IFoS) uses a new four-group framework from CSE 2026 onwards — verify against the latest DoPT policy.</li>
<li><strong>First IAS posting</strong> is typically as an SDM/Assistant Collector after district training.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Step 1 — The Final Result and Merit List</h2>
<p>The process begins when UPSC declares the final result — a merit list of recommended candidates in rank order, based on combined Mains (1,750 marks) and Interview (275 marks) scores. UPSC forwards this list to the Government. At this point you are "recommended for appointment", but several administrative steps must still be completed before you formally become an officer trainee. UPSC also maintains a <strong>consolidated Reserve List</strong> under its rules, from which additional candidates may be recommended if vacancies remain.</p>

<h2>Step 2 — Service Allocation</h2>
<p>Service allocation is handled by DoPT and answers the first big question: <em>which service will you join?</em> It is decided by three factors working together:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Your final rank</strong> — the dominant factor.</li>
<li><strong>Your category</strong> — vacancies are split across UR/EWS/OBC/SC/ST and PwBD.</li>
<li><strong>Your service preferences</strong> — the ordered list you submitted in your Detailed Application Form (DAF-II).</li>
</ul>
<p>These are matched against the <strong>vacancies notified for that year</strong>. Higher-ranked candidates generally receive their top choices — often the IAS, IFS or IPS — while others are allotted various Central Group A services (such as IRS, IAAS, IRTS, IPoS and others) and Group B services. Service allocation happens <strong>first</strong>; only after your service is fixed do All India Service recruits enter cadre allocation.</p>

<h2>Step 3 — Medical Examination and Verification</h2>
<p>Before appointment is confirmed, every recommended candidate must clear:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>A medical examination</strong> at designated government hospitals (in the Delhi region), to confirm fitness against service-specific standards. Physical standards matter especially for services like the IPS.</li>
<li><strong>Document verification</strong> — authentication of educational qualifications, date of birth, category certificates and other credentials.</li>
<li><strong>Character and antecedents / background verification</strong>, conducted through the prescribed agencies.</li>
</ul>
<p>These steps are not formalities to be taken lightly: a failed medical (subject to appeal/review provisions), a discrepancy in documents, or an adverse verification can affect or delay your appointment. Candidates must cooperate fully and submit accurate information.</p>

<h2>Step 4 — Cadre Allocation (for IAS, IPS and IFoS)</h2>
<p>For the three All India Services — IAS, IPS and the Indian Forest Service (IFoS) — a further step decides <em>where</em> in India you will serve: <strong>cadre allocation</strong>. A cadre is the State or Joint Cadre to which an officer is assigned for their career.</p>
<p>Cadre allocation is governed by DoPT policy and balances federal representation with the <strong>insider–outsider principle</strong>: roughly one-third of vacancies in each cadre are filled by "insiders" (home-state candidates) strictly on merit, and the rest by "outsiders" through a roster-based system, preserving the long-standing 1:2 insider–outsider ratio.</p>
<blockquote><p><strong>Important — current rules:</strong> With effect from CSE 2026, the Government replaced the earlier <strong>five-zone system (2017 policy)</strong> with a new <strong>four-group alphabetical framework</strong> that allocates outsider vacancies through a cycle-based rotational roster. This is based on the latest available official UPSC/DoPT cadre allocation policy; candidates must verify the final details from the UPSC CSE 2027 notification and the applicable DoPT Office Memorandum once released, as procedures and timelines (including when cadre preferences are submitted) can change.</p></blockquote>
<p>Cadre allocation for IAS is generally completed early — preferably before the professional course begins — while IPS and IFoS allocations are done soon after appointments are made.</p>

<h2>Step 5 — The Common Foundation Course at LBSNAA</h2>
<p>Now the real transformation begins. All fresh recruits — across the IAS, IPS, IFoS, Indian Foreign Service and various Central Group A services — assemble together at the <a href="https://www.lbsnaa.gov.in/">Lal Bahadur Shastri National Academy of Administration (LBSNAA)</a> in Mussoorie for the common <strong>Foundation Course</strong>.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Duration:</strong> approximately <strong>15 weeks</strong>, typically running from around September to December.</li>
<li><strong>Who attends:</strong> officer trainees of all the above services together — the Foundation Course nominations are made by DoPT to LBSNAA.</li>
<li><strong>What it covers:</strong> basics of public administration, law, economics, the Indian Constitution and management, plus physical training, trekking, village visits and cultural activities.</li>
<li><strong>Why it matters:</strong> its core purpose is to build a <strong>shared understanding of governance and inter-service camaraderie</strong> — so that officers who will later work together across departments already know and trust one another.</li>
</ul>
<p>At current recruitment levels, roughly <strong>650+ officer trainees</strong> undergo the Foundation Course each year at LBSNAA and partner institutions, per the DoPT Training Division. You can read the official description on the <a href="https://trgdiv.dopt.gov.in/FoundationCourse.html">DoPT Training Division Foundation Course page</a> and on the <a href="https://www.lbsnaa.gov.in/menu/function-and-duties-of-organisation">LBSNAA functions page</a>.</p>

<h2>Step 6 — Service-Specific (Professional) Training</h2>
<p>After the Foundation Course, officers move to their own academies for specialised professional training. Here is what that looks like for the major services.</p>

<h3>IAS — LBSNAA, Mussoorie</h3>
<p>IAS officer trainees remain at LBSNAA for professional training in a multi-phase "sandwich" pattern that spans roughly two years overall:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Phase I (~22 weeks):</strong> intensive training in public administration, law, governance, land revenue, development economics and district management, plus the <strong>Bharat Darshan</strong> (Winter Study Tour) across India.</li>
<li><strong>District Training (~52 weeks):</strong> on-the-job attachment in the officer's allotted cadre, working with the district administration, courts, panchayats and field offices under a mentor.</li>
<li><strong>Phase II (~6 weeks):</strong> a concluding module back at LBSNAA to consolidate field experience, culminating in interactions that traditionally include a presentation before the Prime Minister.</li>
</ul>
<p>The official course structure is listed on the <a href="https://www.lbsnaa.gov.in/menu/function-and-duties-of-organisation">LBSNAA website</a>.</p>

<h3>IPS — Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel National Police Academy (SVPNPA), Hyderabad</h3>
<p>IPS probationers train at the <a href="https://www.svpnpa.gov.in/">Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel National Police Academy (SVPNPA)</a> in Hyderabad — the premier police training institution in the country, under the Ministry of Home Affairs:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Basic Course (Phase I, ~11 months):</strong> criminal law (IPC/CrPC and successor laws), forensic science, investigation, weapons handling, tactics, counter-insurgency, physical fitness and leadership.</li>
<li><strong>District Practical Training (~6 months):</strong> field training in the allotted cadre, including police stations, armed battalions and specialised units.</li>
<li><strong>Phase II (~1 month):</strong> a concluding module back at SVPNPA.</li>
</ul>
<p>IPS officers typically take up their first field posting as <strong>Assistant Superintendent of Police (ASP)</strong>.</p>

<h3>IFS (Foreign Service) — Sushma Swaraj Institute of Foreign Service (SSIFS), New Delhi</h3>
<p>Indian Foreign Service officer trainees train at the <a href="https://ssifs.mea.gov.in/">Sushma Swaraj Institute of Foreign Service (SSIFS)</a>, New Delhi, under the Ministry of External Affairs (renamed from the Foreign Service Institute in 2020):</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Induction Training Programme (ITP, ~9 months at SSIFS):</strong> diplomacy, international law, economic and multilateral diplomacy, consular work, defence and cultural diplomacy, plus a desk attachment in divisions of the Ministry of External Affairs.</li>
<li><strong>Compulsory Foreign Language (CFL) posting abroad:</strong> after training in India (about one year including the Foundation Course), officers are posted to a mission abroad as <strong>Third Secretary</strong> to study their assigned language, typically lasting between one and about two-and-a-half years, and must pass a proficiency exam.</li>
</ul>
<p>Details are published on the <a href="https://ssifs.mea.gov.in/itp-for-ifs-ots">SSIFS Induction Training Programme page</a>.</p>

<h3>IRS — NADT (Income Tax) and NACIN (Customs &amp; Indirect Taxes)</h3>
<p>The Indian Revenue Service has two streams, each with its own academy:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>IRS (Income Tax):</strong> trains at the <a href="https://dor.gov.in/nadt">National Academy of Direct Taxes (NADT)</a>, Nagpur — the apex institute under the Central Board of Direct Taxes (CBDT) — in a residential induction programme of about <strong>16 months</strong>, covering direct tax law, accounting and investigation, with field attachments.</li>
<li><strong>IRS (Customs &amp; Indirect Taxes):</strong> trains at the <a href="https://www.nacin.gov.in/">National Academy of Customs, Indirect Taxes &amp; Narcotics (NACIN)</a>, under the Central Board of Indirect Taxes and Customs (CBIC), covering GST, customs and narcotics enforcement.</li>
</ul>
<p>Other Central Group A services similarly have their own dedicated training academies.</p>

<h2>Step 7 — District Training and the First Posting</h2>
<p>For IAS and IPS especially, <strong>district training</strong> is the heart of the probation. This is where classroom knowledge meets ground reality — land revenue and law-and-order systems, scheme implementation, court and field attachments, and shadowing senior officers. It is here that a trainee genuinely learns how administration and policing work at the citizen's doorstep.</p>
<p>After completing all training modules and any prescribed departmental examinations, officers are <strong>confirmed</strong> in service and take up their <strong>first independent posting</strong>:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>IAS:</strong> usually as a <strong>Sub-Divisional Magistrate (SDM)</strong> or Assistant Collector, later rising to District Magistrate/Collector, Secretary-level and higher positions.</li>
<li><strong>IPS:</strong> as an <strong>ASP</strong>, later Superintendent of Police (SP) and senior command roles.</li>
<li><strong>IFS:</strong> as a <strong>Third Secretary</strong> in an Indian mission abroad during language training, progressing through the diplomatic hierarchy.</li>
<li><strong>IRS:</strong> as an <strong>Assistant Commissioner</strong> in tax/customs administration.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Probation, Departmental Exams and Confirmation</h2>
<p>Throughout the training period — commonly referred to as the <strong>probation</strong> — a new officer holds the status of "probationer" or "officer trainee." This period is not merely ceremonial; it carries real evaluation:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Departmental examinations:</strong> officer trainees must pass prescribed departmental/mandatory examinations during training (for example, on law, language, accounts and service rules, depending on the service).</li>
<li><strong>Continuous assessment:</strong> performance in academic modules, physical training and field attachments is assessed at the academy.</li>
<li><strong>Confirmation in service:</strong> after successfully completing training modules and clearing the required examinations, the officer is <strong>confirmed</strong> in the service, moving from probationer to a fully confirmed officer.</li>
</ul>
<p>This structure exists precisely because the roles are consequential — an SDM, an ASP or a diplomat carries genuine public responsibility, so the system verifies readiness before confirmation rather than after the result alone.</p>

<h2>Pay and Facilities During Training</h2>
<p>A common beginner question is whether officer trainees are paid. They are. During training, officer trainees receive a <strong>stipend/salary as government probationers</strong>, and residential accommodation is provided at the academy; trainees typically pay only for mess and certain personal expenses. The exact pay level and allowances are governed by the applicable government pay rules and revisions, so candidates should confirm current figures from official <a href="https://dopt.gov.in/">DoPT</a> and academy sources rather than relying on unofficial numbers. The important point for a beginner is simply this: the moment you become an officer trainee, you are a salaried public servant, not a fee-paying student.</p>

<h2>Beyond the First Posting: How the Career Unfolds</h2>
<p>The first posting is the start of a long, structured career. While the specifics differ by service and cadre, the broad arc for the major services looks like this:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>IAS:</strong> from SDM/Assistant Collector to District Magistrate/Collector, then to state secretariat and, over time, senior positions at state and central levels — including Secretary-rank posts. Mid-career training programmes (MCTP) at LBSNAA punctuate this progression.</li>
<li><strong>IPS:</strong> from ASP to Superintendent of Police (SP), then senior command and leadership roles in state police and central police organisations.</li>
<li><strong>IFS:</strong> from Third Secretary abroad through the diplomatic hierarchy, with postings at Indian missions and at the Ministry of External Affairs headquarters, potentially rising to Ambassador/High Commissioner and Secretary-level roles.</li>
<li><strong>IRS:</strong> from Assistant Commissioner upward through the tax administration, with the direct-tax ladder rising toward the Central Board of Direct Taxes (CBDT) and the indirect-tax ladder toward the Central Board of Indirect Taxes and Customs (CBIC).</li>
</ul>
<p>In-service training does not stop after probation. Officers periodically return for mid-career and specialised courses, and the LBSNAA course list itself includes multiple mid-career training phases for IAS officers — a reminder that a civil servant's learning is genuinely lifelong.</p>

<h2>Common Mistakes and Misconceptions About Life After UPSC</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Thinking the result is the finish line.</strong> It is the start of a demanding, multi-stage training journey and a lifelong career of responsibility.</li>
<li><strong>Confusing service allocation with cadre allocation.</strong> Service (which job) comes first; cadre (which state, for AIS) comes after.</li>
<li><strong>Assuming everyone becomes an IAS.</strong> Only a small share of the final list gets the IAS; most join other equally important services.</li>
<li><strong>Underestimating physical and district training.</strong> Training is rigorous, especially for the IPS, and district training demands real fieldwork.</li>
<li><strong>Relying on outdated cadre rules.</strong> The cadre policy changed for CSE 2026 onwards — always check the current official policy.</li>
<li><strong>Believing training details are fixed forever.</strong> Durations and structures are revised periodically; confirm from official academy and DoPT sources.</li>
</ol>

When you know the journey leads to LBSNAA, a service, a cadre and a life of public impact, the daily study makes deeper sense. Begin with a plan built for the long game.</p>

<h2>Final Summary</h2>
<p>After clearing UPSC, DoPT drives the process: <strong>service allocation</strong> (by rank, category and DAF-II preferences), a <strong>medical examination</strong> and document/background verification, and <strong>cadre allocation</strong> for the All India Services under the new four-group framework (CSE 2026 onwards — verify against the current policy). The result opens the door; the training builds the officer.</p>
