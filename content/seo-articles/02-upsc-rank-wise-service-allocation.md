<BODY>
<p>If you've ever asked <strong>"which rank gets IAS, and which rank gets IPS, IFS or IRS?"</strong>, here's the honest answer that most beginners never hear: <strong>there is no fixed rank for any service.</strong> UPSC first prepares a single merit list. Then the Department of Personnel & Training (DoPT) allocates services <strong>in descending rank order</strong>, giving each candidate the <em>highest service preference for which a vacancy still exists at their turn</em>, within their category. So your final service depends on <strong>four things acting together — your rank, your preference order, your category, and that year's vacancies.</strong> Change any one, and the outcome changes.</p>
<p>That is why a candidate with All-India Rank 60 might become an IPS officer (because they preferred IPS), while someone ranked 85 becomes IAS. This guide explains, in plain language, exactly how service allocation and cadre allocation work — and clears up the myths that mislead thousands of aspirants every year.</p>
<blockquote><p><strong>One rule to remember:</strong> service allocation is <em>relative</em>, not absolute. It is recalculated fresh every year against that year's vacancies and candidate choices. No number on any website — including this one — is a guaranteed cutoff for your attempt.</p></blockquote>

<h2>Key Takeaways (Read This First)</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>UPSC decides your rank; DoPT allocates your service.</strong> Two different steps.</li>
<li>Allocation runs <strong>strictly in rank order</strong>, honouring your highest available preference.</li>
<li>Four factors decide your service: <strong>rank + preference + category + vacancies</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>The same rank can get different services in different years</strong> because vacancies and preferences shift.</li>
<li><strong>Service allocation ≠ cadre allocation.</strong> Cadre allocation (for IAS/IPS/IFoS) decides your <em>state</em>.</li>
<li>From <strong>CSE 2026</strong>, cadre allocation uses a new <strong>four-group</strong> system replacing the old five zones.</li>
<li>Preferences are locked in the <strong>DAF-II</strong>, filled after Mains results and before the interview.</li>
<li>Authentic, year-wise allocation data lives only in <strong>official DoPT lists</strong> — not coaching predictors.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What "Service Allocation" Actually Means</h2>
<p>The UPSC Civil Services Examination is a single exam that recruits for many services at once — the Indian Administrative Service (IAS), the Indian Police Service (IPS), the Indian Foreign Service (IFS), the Indian Revenue Service (IRS), and a range of other Group A and Group B services. But you don't "choose" your service by simply topping the exam. Two distinct steps happen:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>UPSC prepares the final merit list.</strong> Your total of Mains (1750 marks) + Personality Test (275 marks) = 2025 marks decides your All-India Rank. Prelims marks are not counted here.</li>
<li><strong>DoPT allocates services.</strong> Using your rank, your submitted preferences, your category and the year's vacancies, the government assigns you to a specific service.</li>
</ol>
<p>Understanding this split is the first step to setting a realistic target. If the services themselves are still fuzzy to you, read our detailed <a href="/resources/ias-ips-ifs-irs-difference">comparison of IAS, IPS, IFS and IRS</a> before going further — you can't rank preferences well if you don't know what each service does.</p>

<h2>The Four Levers That Decide Your Service</h2>
<p>Every allocation outcome is the product of four levers working together. Master these and the whole system becomes intuitive.</p>

<h3>Lever 1 — Rank</h3>
<p>Your rank fixes your <em>position in the queue</em>. Allocation is processed candidate by candidate, from Rank 1 downward. A higher rank means you reach the front of the line while more services still have open seats. But rank alone does not "give" you a service — it only decides <em>when your turn comes</em>.</p>

<h3>Lever 2 — Preference (your DAF-II order)</h3>
<p>After the Mains result, shortlisted candidates fill the <strong>Detailed Application Form II (DAF-II)</strong>, in which they list the services in their personal order of preference. When your turn comes, the system gives you the <strong>highest-preference service that still has a vacancy</strong>. This is why a Rank-60 candidate who lists IPS above IAS will receive IPS — the system respects your stated choice.</p>
<blockquote><p><strong>Mentor caution:</strong> preferences are powerful and irreversible for that cycle. If you rank a service low, you may not get it even at a good rank; if you rank it high, you may get it "early". Fill your DAF-II deliberately, not casually.</p></blockquote>

<h3>Lever 3 — Category</h3>
<p>Allocation respects the reservation framework. Separate consideration is given to Unreserved (UR), OBC, SC, ST and EWS vacancies as per the roster. Because each category has its own set of vacancies, the <strong>rank required for the same service differs across categories</strong>. This is a feature of the constitutional reservation system, not an anomaly. (For how categories affect who can even apply, see our <a href="/resources/upsc-eligibility-criteria-2027">UPSC eligibility guide</a>.)</p>

<h3>Lever 4 — Vacancies</h3>
<p>The number of seats in each service is fixed afresh every year by the cadre-controlling authorities, and the total is announced with the notification (and can be revised). More IAS vacancies in a given year relax the effective cutoff; fewer tighten it. Vacancies are the moving floor beneath the whole exercise.</p>

<h2>How the Allocation Runs, Step by Step</h2>
<ol>
<li>UPSC declares the final result and the <strong>All-India merit list</strong>.</li>
<li>DoPT compiles the <strong>vacancy matrix</strong> — service-wise and category-wise seats for that year.</li>
<li>Starting from Rank 1, each candidate is matched to their <strong>highest-preference service with an available vacancy</strong>, within their category's roster.</li>
<li>This is published as a <strong>service-allocation list</strong>, often in multiple "iterations" as documentary/eligibility/medical checks are completed and some candidates move up.</li>
<li>Seats that fall vacant (e.g., a reserved-category candidate selected on general merit later claiming their category) are filled using the <strong>Reserve List</strong>, maintained confidentially under the Civil Services Examination Rules.</li>
</ol>
<p>The asterisks you sometimes see next to names in an allocation list flag candidates who may be <strong>upgraded</strong> in a later iteration — for instance, the first IPS allottee just after the last IAS seat, who moves to IAS if someone above withdraws.</p>

<h2>Rank Bands: A Reality Check (Illustrative, Not a Promise)</h2>
<p>Aspirants constantly search for "the rank for IAS". It's natural to want a number — but stating one as fact would be misleading. What we <em>can</em> say is directional, based on publicly documented patterns from past years:</p>
<ul>
<li>In the <strong>general/unreserved</strong> category, IAS has historically closed within roughly the <strong>top ~100 ranks</strong>, IFS (which has very few seats) even higher up, IPS extending several hundred ranks down, and the IRS wings and other Group A services further down the list.</li>
<li>For <strong>reserved categories</strong>, the same service is secured at a numerically higher (lower-merit) rank, reflecting category-wise vacancies.</li>
</ul>
<blockquote><p>⚠️ These are <strong>indicative historical patterns, not cutoffs for your year</strong>. Vacancies swing sharply between years, which moves the effective cutoff significantly. Treat any specific "last rank" you see online as a rough reference, and rely on the <strong>official DoPT allocation lists</strong> for authentic year-wise figures. Do not plan your preferences around a single number.</p></blockquote>
<p>The practical takeaway for a beginner is simple: <strong>aim to maximise your marks and rank</strong>, and treat the service as an outcome of rank + smart preference-filling — not the other way around.</p>

<h2>A Worked Example (Hypothetical, to Show the Logic)</h2>
<p>Numbers here are <strong>illustrative only</strong> — invented to demonstrate the mechanism, not real cutoffs. Imagine a year with, say, 180 IAS seats and a general-category candidate pool, and three candidates:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Rank 3 — prefers IFS first, IAS second.</strong> When their turn comes, IFS seats are open, so they get <strong>IFS</strong>, even though IAS is also available. Their IAS "slot" effectively passes to someone below.</li>
<li><strong>Rank 45 — prefers IAS first.</strong> IAS seats are still open at Rank 45, so they get <strong>IAS</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>Rank 210 (general) — prefers IAS first, but IAS general seats are already filled.</strong> The system moves to their next available preference with an open vacancy — perhaps <strong>IPS</strong> or an IRS wing.</li>
</ul>
<p>Now change one variable — say IAS vacancies rise sharply that year, or several top-rankers prefer IFS/IPS over IAS. The IAS "last rank" instantly shifts. That single thought experiment explains why chasing a fixed number is futile, and why <strong>marks (rank) + smart preference order</strong> are what you can actually control.</p>

<h2>Where the Vacancy Numbers Come From</h2>
<p>The vacancy matrix isn't arbitrary. Cadre-controlling authorities (DoPT for IAS, MHA for IPS, MoEFCC for IFoS, and the respective ministries for central services) determine service-wise and category-wise vacancies as per established procedure, and the expected total is announced with the examination notification. For the All India Services, cadre-level gaps are calculated with reference to a cut-off date, and state governments must communicate their requirements within prescribed timelines; requisitions received late are not counted for that year. The final number of vacancies can be revised after firm inputs are received. This is why the notification's vacancy figure is described as "expected" or "approximate" until finalised.</p>

<h2>Service Allocation vs. Cadre Allocation: Don't Confuse Them</h2>
<p>These two terms are used interchangeably by beginners, but they are different stages.</p>
<table>
<thead><tr><th>Aspect</th><th>Service allocation</th><th>Cadre allocation</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>What it decides</td><td>Which <strong>service</strong> (IAS, IPS, IFS, IRS…)</td><td>Which <strong>state cadre</strong> you serve in</td></tr>
<tr><td>Applies to</td><td>All selected candidates</td><td>The All India Services: <strong>IAS, IPS, IFoS</strong></td></tr>
<tr><td>Decided by</td><td>DoPT, on rank + preference + category + vacancy</td><td>DoPT/MHA/MoEFCC under the Cadre Allocation Policy</td></tr>
<tr><td>Key principle</td><td>Highest available preference in rank order</td><td>Merit-in-category + insider–outsider + vacancies</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<p>Officers in the IRS and other central services are not "cadre-allocated" to a state the way IAS/IPS/IFoS officers are; their postings follow their own department's structure across the country.</p>

<h2>Cadre Allocation for IAS/IPS/IFoS (New 2026 Policy)</h2>
<p>For the three All India Services, once your service is fixed, a second exercise decides <em>where in India you will serve</em> — your state cadre. This is governed by the DoPT Cadre Allocation Policy, and it was <strong>substantially revised with effect from CSE 2026</strong>.</p>
<h3>What changed</h3>
<ul>
<li>The earlier <strong>five-zone</strong> system (under the 2017 policy) has been <strong>replaced by four alphabetical groups</strong> of state and joint cadres.</li>
<li>Allocation now uses a <strong>rule-based, rotational cycle</strong> across these four groups, with the starting group rotating each year to keep the distribution balanced.</li>
<li>The <strong>cadre-controlling authorities</strong> remain: DoPT for IAS, the Ministry of Home Affairs (MHA) for IPS, and the Ministry of Environment, Forest & Climate Change (MoEFCC) for IFoS.</li>
</ul>
<h3>How cadre allocation works in principle</h3>
<ol>
<li>Cadre-controlling authorities determine <strong>vacancies</strong> for each cadre, split into UR/OBC/SC/ST and insider/outsider.</li>
<li>Candidates indicate cadre preferences as required by the policy.</li>
<li><strong>Insider vacancies</strong> (home cadre) are filled first, strictly by merit within category, for candidates who opted for their home cadre and are eligible.</li>
<li>Remaining <strong>outsider vacancies</strong> are filled using the group-wise rotational mechanism.</li>
<li>EWS is adjusted within the UR pool; it does not create a separate cadre-vacancy stream.</li>
</ol>
<p>Because this policy was recently overhauled, its finer mechanics (grouping details, cycle order, willingness rules) should be read from the official DoPT document rather than older explainers. The governing rules and the actual allocation lists are published by <a href="https://dopt.gov.in" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">DoPT</a> on its official portal.</p>

<h2>The Reserve List and Iterations</h2>
<p>Two features often confuse newcomers watching the results roll out:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Iterations:</strong> the allocation list is released in stages. Between iterations, candidates can be upgraded as vacancies clear document verification, category claims and medical checks.</li>
<li><strong>Reserve List:</strong> maintained confidentially by UPSC under the Civil Services Examination Rules, it holds candidates just below the last recommended candidate, category-wise. If seats remain unfilled after allocation — for example when a reserved-category candidate selected on general merit later claims their category and vacates a UR seat — the Reserve List is used to fill the gap.</li>
</ul>
<p>The key point: your allocation isn't always final on day one, and small upward movements are normal.</p>

<h2>Common Myths About Service Allocation</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Myth: "Rank 1 always becomes IAS."</strong> Usually yes, because most toppers prefer IAS — but it's the <em>preference</em>, not an automatic rule. A topper who prefers IFS gets IFS.</li>
<li><strong>Myth: "There's a fixed rank for IAS every year."</strong> No. The effective cutoff moves with vacancies and preferences, and differs by category.</li>
<li><strong>Myth: "IPS is only for candidates who couldn't get IAS."</strong> No. Many strong candidates <em>prefer</em> IPS and rank it above IAS. Preference reflects aspiration, not just rank.</li>
<li><strong>Myth: "My category guarantees me IAS at any rank."</strong> No. Category has its own limited vacancies; you still compete within your category's merit list.</li>
<li><strong>Myth: "Cadre allocation depends on service preference."</strong> No. Once your service is fixed, cadre allocation is a separate exercise with its own rules (merit-in-category, insider–outsider, groups).</li>
<li><strong>Myth: "Online rank-predictors are accurate."</strong> They're indicative at best. Only official DoPT lists are authentic, and even those describe the past, not your future year.</li>
</ul>

</ol>

<h2>A Note for Reserved-Category Aspirants</h2>
<p>If you belong to OBC, SC, ST or EWS, an important nuance is worth understanding early. Reserved-category candidates who are selected on <strong>general merit</strong> (i.e., their rank is good enough to be counted against unreserved seats without availing any relaxation) are generally adjusted against the unreserved pool, while those availing category benefits are counted against category vacancies. This interaction — between merit, category and the reservation roster — is exactly why the "last rank" for the same service differs across categories and why category candidates sometimes vacate a UR seat that is then filled from the Reserve List. None of this changes the core rule: allocation is still processed strictly in rank order within the applicable pool, honouring your DAF preferences. The practical implication is simply that you should know which pool your rank places you in and set expectations accordingly.</p>

<h2>Final Summary</h2>
<p>Service allocation is not a lottery and not a fixed table — it's a transparent, rank-ordered matching of candidates to services based on <strong>rank, preference, category and vacancies</strong>. UPSC builds the merit list; DoPT does the allocation. Because vacancies and preferences change annually, the same rank can yield different services in different years, and there is <strong>no guaranteed cutoff</strong> for any service. For the All India Services, a separate <strong>cadre allocation</strong> — revised into a four-group system from CSE 2026 — then decides your state, using merit-in-category and the insider–outsider principle. Focus your energy on maximising marks and understanding the services, then fill your DAF-II with intent. For authentic numbers, always go to the official DoPT lists.</p>
