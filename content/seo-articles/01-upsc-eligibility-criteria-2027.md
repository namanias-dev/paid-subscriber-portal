<BODY>
<p>If you are wondering <strong>"Am I even eligible to apply for the UPSC Civil Services Examination?"</strong>, here is the short answer first. For a general-category candidate, you broadly need three things: a <strong>graduate degree</strong> from a recognised university (any subject), an age <strong>between 21 and 32 years</strong> as on 1 August of the exam year, and <strong>Indian citizenship</strong> for the IAS, IPS and Indian Foreign Service. You get up to <strong>six attempts</strong>, with extra attempts and age relaxation for reserved categories. Final-year students can also apply. That's the essence — the rest of this guide unpacks every condition, category by category, so you can check your eligibility with confidence.</p>
<p>The eligibility rules for the UPSC CSE are defined every year in the <em>official examination notification</em>, and they have been remarkably stable for years. This article explains that long-standing framework in plain language, flags exactly where you must confirm the current-year figures, and gives you a printable checklist at the end.</p>
<blockquote><p><strong>Important note on 2027:</strong> This is based on the latest available official UPSC Civil Services Examination notification/rules; candidates must verify the final details from the UPSC CSE 2027 notification once it is released. Do not treat any coaching website's numbers — including this page — as the final word for your specific attempt.</p></blockquote>

<h2>Key Takeaways (Read This First)</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Education:</strong> a bachelor's degree in <em>any</em> discipline from a recognised university. No minimum percentage.</li>
<li><strong>Age:</strong> 21 to 32 years for the general category, calculated <strong>as on 1 August of the exam year</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>Attempts:</strong> 6 for General/EWS, 9 for OBC, unlimited for SC/ST (within their age limit).</li>
<li><strong>Category relaxation:</strong> +3 years for OBC, +5 for SC/ST, +10 for PwBD, plus defence/ex-servicemen relaxations.</li>
<li><strong>Nationality:</strong> Indian citizen for IAS/IPS/IFS; wider categories allowed for some other services.</li>
<li><strong>Final-year students</strong> can appear for Prelims but must prove they passed graduation before Mains.</li>
<li><strong>EWS</strong> gives reservation but <em>no</em> extra age relaxation or attempts.</li>
<li>Always cross-check against the <a href="https://upsc.gov.in/exams-related-info/exam-notification" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">official UPSC examination notification page</a> for your target year.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The Three Pillars of UPSC Eligibility</h2>
<p>Every eligibility condition for the Civil Services Examination falls under one of three heads: <strong>nationality</strong>, <strong>educational qualification</strong>, and <strong>age & attempts</strong>. A fourth condition — <strong>medical/physical fitness</strong> — applies at the appointment stage. If you satisfy all four, you can apply. Let's take them one at a time.</p>

<h2>1. Nationality: Who Can Apply?</h2>
<p>Nationality rules differ depending on <em>which service</em> you are aiming for. This is one of the most misunderstood parts of UPSC eligibility, so read it carefully.</p>
<h3>For IAS, IPS and the Indian Foreign Service (IFS)</h3>
<p>You <strong>must be a citizen of India</strong>. There is no alternative. These three services are open only to Indian citizens.</p>
<h3>For other services (e.g., IRS, IA&AS, IRTS and other Group A/B services)</h3>
<p>A candidate must be one of the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>a citizen of India, or</li>
<li>a subject of Nepal, or</li>
<li>a subject of Bhutan, or</li>
<li>a Tibetan refugee who came to India before 1 January 1962 with the intention of permanently settling in India, or</li>
<li>a person of Indian origin who has migrated from Pakistan, Burma (Myanmar), Sri Lanka, or the East African countries of Kenya, Uganda, the United Republic of Tanzania, Zambia, Malawi, Zaire, Ethiopia and Vietnam, with the intention of permanently settling in India.</li>
</ul>
<p>Candidates in the last four categories must possess a <strong>Certificate of Eligibility</strong> issued by the Government of India. Such a candidate may be admitted to the examination, but an offer of appointment will be given only after the certificate has been issued. In practice, the vast majority of aspirants are Indian citizens and this nuance does not affect them — but it matters if you fall into one of the special categories.</p>
<blockquote><p><strong>Mentor tip:</strong> because IAS, IPS and IFS require Indian citizenship, if you are not a citizen you should factor that into your <a href="/resources/upsc-rank-wise-service-allocation">service preference planning</a> from the start.</p></blockquote>

<h2>2. Educational Qualification: Do You Have the Right Degree?</h2>
<p>The educational bar is deliberately broad and inclusive — this is why students from every stream, from engineering to arts to medicine, sit for the exam.</p>
<ul>
<li>You must hold a <strong>graduate degree</strong> from a university incorporated by an Act of the Central or State legislature in India, or from an institution established by an Act of Parliament, or one declared a deemed university under Section 3 of the University Grants Commission (UGC) Act, 1956 — <strong>or an equivalent qualification</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>Any discipline is accepted.</strong> B.A., B.Sc., B.Com., B.Tech./B.E., MBBS, LLB, and other recognised degrees all qualify.</li>
<li><strong>No minimum percentage is prescribed.</strong> A simple pass degree meets the requirement.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Special cases the notification recognises</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Professional and technical qualifications</strong> recognised by the Government as equivalent to a professional or technical degree are accepted.</li>
<li><strong>Medical students</strong> who have passed the final MBBS examination but have not completed their internship can apply, provided they submit a certificate from the concerned authority that they have passed the final exam (the internship completion certificate is submitted later, as specified).</li>
</ul>

<h3>The final-year student rule (very important)</h3>
<p>This is one of the most common beginner questions. <strong>Yes, final-year students can apply.</strong> Here's how it works:</p>
<ul>
<li>Candidates who are in the <strong>final year of their degree</strong> (or awaiting results) can apply and appear for the <strong>Preliminary Examination</strong>.</li>
<li>However, they <strong>must submit proof of passing the required qualifying examination along with the application for the Main Examination.</strong></li>
<li>If a candidate clears Prelims but cannot produce that proof at the Mains stage, their candidature is cancelled.</li>
</ul>
<p>In effect, this lets you start your UPSC journey while still in college. Many toppers take their first serious attempt in their graduating year. If that's you, understand the exam architecture early with our <a href="/resources/upsc-exam-pattern-syllabus">UPSC exam pattern and syllabus guide</a> and build a realistic <a href="/resources/upsc-study-plan-for-beginners">study plan for beginners</a>.</p>

<h2>3. Age Limit: Are You in the Window?</h2>
<p>Age is calculated <strong>as on 1 August of the year of examination</strong> — not on the date of application, Prelims, Mains or interview. This single reference date decides both your minimum and maximum age.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Minimum age:</strong> you must have attained <strong>21 years</strong> as on 1 August of the exam year. This applies to <em>all</em> categories.</li>
<li><strong>Maximum age (general category):</strong> you must <em>not</em> have attained <strong>32 years</strong> as on that date.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Category-wise age relaxation</h3>
<p>The upper age limit is relaxed for specified categories. The table below shows the long-standing framework. These figures have been consistent across recent notifications, but you must reconfirm them for your target year.</p>
<table>
<thead><tr><th>Category</th><th>Upper age relaxation</th><th>Effective upper age</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>General / EWS</td><td>No relaxation</td><td>32 years</td></tr>
<tr><td>OBC (non-creamy layer)</td><td>+3 years</td><td>35 years</td></tr>
<tr><td>SC / ST</td><td>+5 years</td><td>37 years</td></tr>
<tr><td>Persons with Benchmark Disabilities (PwBD)</td><td>+10 years</td><td>42 (Gen/EWS), 45 (OBC), 47 (SC/ST)*</td></tr>
<tr><td>Defence Services Personnel (disabled in operations)</td><td>+3 years</td><td>as applicable</td></tr>
<tr><td>Ex-servicemen (incl. ECOs/SSCOs, conditions apply)</td><td>+5 years</td><td>as applicable</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<p><em>*The PwBD relaxation of 10 years is added on top of any SC/ST/OBC relaxation the candidate is also entitled to, giving the higher effective ages shown.</em></p>
<p>A crucial official rule: candidates who belong to SC/ST/OBC <em>and</em> are also covered under another clause (such as ex-servicemen or PwBD) are eligible for <strong>cumulative age relaxation</strong> under both categories. The exact list of relaxations, and the definition of terms like "ex-servicemen", is set out in the notification.</p>
<blockquote><p>⚠️ <strong>PwBD appointment caveat:</strong> age relaxation does not automatically guarantee appointment. PwBD candidates are considered eligible for appointment only if, after the prescribed medical examination, they satisfy the physical and medical standards for the services to be allocated to them. Confirm the specifics in the official notification.</p></blockquote>

<h2>4. Number of Attempts</h2>
<p>The number of attempts is the rule that most affects long-term planning, so understand it precisely.</p>
<table>
<thead><tr><th>Category</th><th>Number of attempts</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>General</td><td>6</td></tr>
<tr><td>EWS</td><td>6</td></tr>
<tr><td>OBC</td><td>9</td></tr>
<tr><td>SC / ST</td><td>Unlimited (up to the age limit)</td></tr>
<tr><td>PwBD (General / EWS / OBC)</td><td>9</td></tr>
<tr><td>PwBD (SC / ST)</td><td>Unlimited (up to the age limit)</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<h3>What counts as an "attempt"?</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Appearing in the Preliminary Examination counts as one attempt.</strong> If you appear in even one Prelims paper, it is counted.</li>
<li><strong>Merely applying — but not appearing — does not count.</strong></li>
<li>If your candidature is disqualified/cancelled, the rules on whether that attempt counts are set out in the notification; check them if this applies to you.</li>
</ul>
<p>Note that attempts and age work together: SC/ST candidates have "unlimited" attempts, but only until they cross their upper age limit (37, or higher with cumulative relaxation). So the age ceiling is the real outer boundary for everyone.</p>

<h2>5. Medical & Physical Standards (Appointment Stage)</h2>
<p>Clearing the written exam and interview is not the last hurdle. Recommended candidates must undergo a <strong>medical examination</strong> conducted as per the standards prescribed by the Government for the services concerned. A few points beginners should know:</p>
<ul>
<li>General health, vision, and fitness standards apply across services, with the details in the notification and its appendices.</li>
<li>Some services — most notably the <strong>IPS</strong> — have <strong>additional physical standards</strong> such as minimum height, chest measurement and eyesight requirements, which differ for male and female candidates and for certain categories (e.g., ST candidates and candidates from specified regions).</li>
<li>PwBD candidates are allocated only those services in which they meet the required physical and medical standards.</li>
</ul>
<p>Because these standards are detailed and service-specific, do not rely on second-hand summaries. Read the medical standards section of the official notification if you have any specific health concern, and consult a doctor where needed.</p>

<h2>Reservation Categories Explained (EWS / OBC / SC / ST / PwBD)</h2>
<p>Reservation affects <strong>three separate things</strong>: eligibility relaxations, the fee, and the final allocation of vacancies. Keep them distinct in your mind.</p>
<table>
<thead><tr><th>Category</th><th>Age relaxation</th><th>Extra attempts</th><th>Vacancy reservation</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>EWS</td><td>No</td><td>No</td><td>Yes (within UR pool)</td></tr>
<tr><td>OBC (NCL)</td><td>Yes (+3)</td><td>Yes (9)</td><td>Yes</td></tr>
<tr><td>SC</td><td>Yes (+5)</td><td>Unlimited</td><td>Yes</td></tr>
<tr><td>ST</td><td>Yes (+5)</td><td>Unlimited</td><td>Yes</td></tr>
<tr><td>PwBD</td><td>Yes (+10)</td><td>9 / Unlimited*</td><td>Yes</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<p><em>*PwBD attempts follow the candidate's social category as explained above.</em></p>
<p>A frequent point of confusion: <strong>EWS is not the same as OBC/SC/ST for relaxations.</strong> The 10% EWS reservation (introduced by the 103rd Constitutional Amendment) gives you a reserved slice of vacancies, but it does <em>not</em> give you a higher age limit or extra attempts. EWS candidates follow the general-category age (32) and attempts (6). To claim any reservation, you must hold a valid category certificate in the prescribed format and, for OBC, be in the <strong>non-creamy layer</strong>. How your category then interacts with rank and vacancies is explained in our guide to <a href="/resources/upsc-rank-wise-service-allocation">rank-wise service allocation</a>.</p>

<h2>Does Your Degree Type or Background Affect Eligibility?</h2>
<p>Beginners worry endlessly about edge cases. Here's how the common ones are treated under the standard framework:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Distance/open-university or correspondence degrees:</strong> a degree from a university recognised as above (incorporated by law, established by an Act of Parliament, or a UGC-recognised/deemed university) is acceptable. The <em>mode</em> of study is not the deciding factor; the <em>recognition of the awarding institution</em> is.</li>
<li><strong>Engineering, medical, law, commerce, arts — any stream:</strong> all recognised bachelor's degrees qualify equally. Your graduation subject does not restrict which service you can target.</li>
<li><strong>Gap years / age of your degree:</strong> there is no rule that your degree must be recent. What matters is that you hold it (or will, before Mains) and that you fall within your category's age window as on 1 August of the exam year.</li>
<li><strong>Multiple attempts already used elsewhere:</strong> attempts are counted only for the UPSC Civil Services Examination itself; appearing in other exams (SSC, state PSCs, etc.) does not consume your CSE attempts.</li>
</ul>
<p>If your specific situation isn't covered here, the safest course is to read the qualification clause of the current notification and, if still unsure, seek clarification before applying.</p>

<h2>Documents & Proof You Should Keep Ready</h2>
<p>Eligibility is not just about meeting the conditions — you also have to <em>prove</em> them at the right stage. Keep these ready in valid form:</p>
<ul>
<li>Proof of <strong>date of birth</strong> (matriculation/secondary certificate is typically used).</li>
<li>Proof of <strong>educational qualification</strong> (degree/provisional certificate; final-year students submit proof of passing before Mains).</li>
<li>A valid <strong>category certificate</strong> in the prescribed format, if claiming OBC (non-creamy layer), SC, ST, EWS or PwBD benefits, dated as required.</li>
<li>Any <strong>ex-servicemen / defence</strong> documents, if claiming those relaxations.</li>
</ul>
<p>Mismatched names, expired certificates, or wrong formats are among the most common reasons candidates lose category benefits or face candidature issues — so verify these early, not at the last minute.</p>

<h2>Restrictions Every Aspirant Should Know</h2>
<p>Beyond the positive conditions, the notification also lists certain restrictions. The most relevant for beginners:</p>
<ul>
<li>A candidate already <strong>appointed to the IAS or the Indian Foreign Service</strong> on the basis of an earlier examination, and continuing in that service, is generally <strong>not eligible</strong> to sit again (subject to the exact wording of the current notification).</li>
<li>Candidates must be of good moral character and eligible in every respect on the date they are due to appear/appointed.</li>
<li>Furnishing false information or suppressing material facts can lead to disqualification.</li>
</ul>
<p>These provisions vary in wording year to year, so read the "restrictions" section of the current notification carefully if you are already in service.</p>

<h2>Where Beginners Get Eligibility Wrong (Common Mistakes)</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Miscalculating the age reference date.</strong> Age is counted as on <strong>1 August of the exam year</strong>, not the application date. People wrongly assume they've "aged out" — or that they still have a year — and get it backwards.</li>
<li><strong>Assuming EWS gives extra attempts.</strong> It doesn't. Only OBC/SC/ST (and PwBD by social category) get extra attempts.</li>
<li><strong>Thinking you need a specific degree or high marks.</strong> Any recognised graduate degree in any stream, with no minimum percentage, qualifies.</li>
<li><strong>Believing you cannot apply as a final-year student.</strong> You can appear for Prelims; you only need proof of passing before Mains.</li>
<li><strong>Confusing "applied" with "attempt".</strong> Only <em>appearing</em> in Prelims consumes an attempt.</li>
<li><strong>Relying on outdated blog numbers.</strong> Rules can change; the binding source is always the current official notification.</li>
<li><strong>Ignoring the OBC non-creamy-layer and certificate-validity rules.</strong> An expired or wrongly-dated certificate can cost you your category benefits.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The Two Official Documents You Must Trust</h2>
<p>For eligibility, only two official sources matter:</p>
<ol>
<li>The <strong>official UPSC examination notification</strong> for your target year — the binding legal document with the exact age, attempts, education, nationality and medical rules. Read it in full the year you apply, from the <a href="https://upsc.gov.in/exams-related-info/exam-notification" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">official UPSC notifications page</a>.</li>
<li>The <strong>UPSC online application portal</strong>, where you actually register and can see the eligibility declarations you must certify: <a href="https://upsconline.gov.in" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">upsconline.gov.in</a>.</li>
</ol>
<p>You can also track the annual schedule on the <a href="https://upsc.gov.in/examinations/exam-calendar" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">official UPSC exam calendar</a>. For the recruitment rules and cadre framework that flow from eligibility, the <a href="https://dopt.gov.in" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Department of Personnel & Training (DoPT)</a> is the parent authority.</p>

<h2>Final Summary</h2>
<p>UPSC eligibility comes down to four checks: a <strong>recognised graduate degree</strong> in any subject, an <strong>age within your category's window</strong> (21–32 for general, relaxed for reserved categories) as on 1 August of the exam year, the right <strong>nationality</strong> (Indian citizen for IAS/IPS/IFS), and — at the appointment stage — <strong>medical fitness</strong>. General/EWS get 6 attempts, OBC 9, and SC/ST unlimited within the age limit. Final-year students can apply. EWS gives reservation but no age/attempt relaxation. These rules have been stable for years, but the binding version for your attempt is always the current official notification.</p>
