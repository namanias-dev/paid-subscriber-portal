<h2>Central Sector vs Centrally Sponsored: A Distinction UPSC Loves</h2>
<p>One of the most reliable Prelims and interview points is the classification of a scheme. A <strong>Central Sector Scheme (CSS-Central)</strong> is fully funded and implemented by the Union government, usually on Union List subjects — examples above include PM Vishwakarma, PM-Vidyalaxmi, One Nation One Subscription and Namo Drone Didi. A <strong>Centrally Sponsored Scheme (CSS)</strong> is jointly funded by the Centre and states in a defined ratio and typically implemented by the states, often on Concurrent or State List subjects — flagship examples include PMAY and the health-assurance components of Ayushman Bharat. The funding pattern (100% Union vs shared) is the single fact that most often distinguishes the two in an MCQ, so tag every scheme you learn with its type.</p>
<p>A related nuance worth remembering: some initiatives are <strong>"missions" or "abhiyans" built on convergence</strong> rather than standalone schemes. PM-JANMAN, for instance, is not a single scheme but an umbrella mission delivering 11 interventions through nine line ministries with the Ministry of Tribal Affairs as nodal — itself a governance model worth citing in a Mains answer on whole-of-government delivery.</p>

</ul>
<p>When you revise a scheme, jot down the two or three GS themes it can serve. This turns a static fact into a flexible answer-writing asset.</p>

<h2>How to Use These Schemes in Prelims</h2>
<p>UPSC Prelims tests schemes in three recurring formats. First, <strong>matching a scheme to its ministry</strong> — this is why the ministry column above is the single most important thing to memorise. Second, <strong>statement-based questions</strong> on features ("With reference to Scheme X, consider the following statements…") — here the funding pattern, target group and eligibility limits are tested. Third, <strong>fact anchors</strong> such as outlay, coverage numbers or the year of launch, usually as a distractor. Your revision method should therefore be:</p>
<ul>
<li>Cover the ministry column and test recall from the scheme name.</li>
<li>Note whether each scheme is <strong>Central Sector</strong> (fully Union-funded) or <strong>Centrally Sponsored</strong> (Centre-state shared) — a classic distractor.</li>
<li>Fix one distinctive feature per scheme (e.g., "PM-Vidyalaxmi = top 860 QHEIs, 3% interest subvention up to ₹10 lakh").</li>
<li>Do not over-memorise exact rupee figures for every scheme; know orders of magnitude and the standout numbers UPSC is likely to test.</li>
</ul>

<h2>How to Use These Schemes in Mains</h2>
<p>In Mains, a scheme should never appear as a bare name. Use the <strong>"theme → design → evidence → gap → way-forward"</strong> frame:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Theme:</strong> place the scheme in the right GS bucket — PM-JANMAN in tribal/social justice (GS-I &amp; GS-II), PM Surya Ghar in energy transition (GS-III), Ayushman Vay Vandana in health &amp; ageing (GS-II).</li>
<li><strong>Design:</strong> comment on targeting, funding pattern and convergence (e.g., PM-JANMAN's 9-ministry convergence model is itself a governance point).</li>
<li><strong>Evidence:</strong> cite the scheme as concrete proof of a policy direction rather than an abstract claim.</li>
<li><strong>Gap:</strong> add one honest limitation — uptake, exclusion errors, DISCOM/fiscal stress, or implementation capacity.</li>
<li><strong>Way-forward:</strong> close with a reform or convergence suggestion.</li>
</ul>
<p>One scheme can serve multiple answers: Namo Drone Didi is simultaneously about <em>women's empowerment</em>, <em>technology in agriculture</em> and <em>SHG-led livelihoods</em>. Build these cross-links once and reuse them. Our <a href="/resources/upsc-mains-answer-writing">Mains answer-writing guide</a> shows how to weave scheme examples into a structured answer.</p>

<h2>Common Mistakes Aspirants Make with Schemes</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Learning schemes as an unstructured list</strong> instead of ministry-wise — this is why matching questions feel impossible in the exam.</li>
<li><strong>Confusing Central Sector with Centrally Sponsored</strong> schemes — a frequent distractor.</li>
<li><strong>Memorising outdated details</strong> from old PDFs (wrong ministry after a reshuffle, or a scheme that has since been subsumed).</li>
<li><strong>Naming schemes in Mains without analysis</strong> — no theme, no gap, no way-forward.</li>
<li><strong>Trying to cover every scheme ever launched</strong> rather than mastering the high-yield 40-60.</li>
<li><strong>Trusting unofficial sources</strong> for the ministry name or outlay instead of PIB/ministry pages.</li>
