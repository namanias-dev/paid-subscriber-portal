<BODY>
<p>The newspaper is the most over-consumed and under-used resource in UPSC preparation. Beginners spend two hours highlighting every headline, feel productive, and remember almost nothing that's exam-relevant. Meanwhile, a trained aspirant reads the same paper in <strong>45 focused minutes</strong> and walks away with three answer-ready issues.</p>
<p>The difference isn't speed-reading — it's <strong>knowing what to read, what to skip, and what to do with what you read.</strong> This guide gives you exactly that: a time-boxed method for The Hindu or Indian Express, the read/skip filter, how to read for <em>issues</em> rather than <em>events</em>, and how to build a single current-affairs note that actually feeds your Prelims and Mains.</p>
<blockquote><p><strong>Why it matters:</strong> current affairs sits inside Prelims <em>and</em> every GS Mains paper. The newspaper is also your best <a href="/resources/upsc-mains-answer-writing">answer-writing example bank</a>.</p></blockquote>

<h2>Key Takeaways (Read This First)</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>45–60 minutes, fixed.</strong> Time-box it or it expands to swallow your day.</li>
<li><strong>Read for issues, not events.</strong> The debate matters; the daily incident usually doesn't.</li>
<li><strong>One newspaper, read well</strong> — not two skimmed. Add PIB for schemes.</li>
<li><strong>One topic-wise note,</strong> organised by GS theme, revised monthly.</li>
<li><strong>Link every article to the syllabus.</strong> If it serves no syllabus line, skip it.</li>
<li><strong>Consistency beats intensity.</strong> Every day at 45 minutes beats a 3-hour Sunday binge.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Issues, Not Events: The Core Mindset</h2>
<p>UPSC rarely asks "what happened on Tuesday." It asks about the <strong>underlying issue</strong>: the policy, the debate, the institutional question, the trade-off. So when you read about a new bill, a court verdict or a scheme, don't memorise the event — extract the <em>issue</em>: What problem does it address? What are the arguments for and against? Which constitutional/economic/environmental principle is at stake? That's what becomes a Prelims option or a Mains point.</p>

<h2>What to Read (Priority Order)</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Editorials &amp; Op-Eds</strong> — the heart of the paper for UPSC; they model balanced argument for Mains and essays.</li>
<li><strong>National / Governance &amp; Polity</strong> — bills, institutions, Supreme Court judgements, welfare schemes, federal issues.</li>
<li><strong>Economy</strong> — policy, budgets, RBI, reports, indicators (skip stock-market ticker noise).</li>
<li><strong>Environment &amp; Science-Tech</strong> — climate, biodiversity, energy, health, space, digital governance.</li>
<li><strong>International Relations</strong> — India's bilateral/multilateral ties, groupings, neighbourhood.</li>
<li><strong>The Explained/Analysis pages</strong> — issue-focused pieces built for exactly this purpose.</li>
</ol>

<h2>What to Skip (Guilt-Free)</h2>
<ul>
<li>Celebrity, entertainment and lifestyle.</li>
<li>Most sport (keep only major national achievements/awards).</li>
<li>Local crime and routine city news.</li>
<li>Political mud-slinging and personality clashes (read the <em>policy</em>, not the theatrics).</li>
<li>Opinion pieces with no syllabus connection.</li>
</ul>
<blockquote><p><strong>Mentor note:</strong> skipping is a skill. The confidence to <em>not</em> read something is what compresses three hours into forty-five minutes. Trust the syllabus filter.</p></blockquote>

<h2>The 45-Minute Method (Step by Step)</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Scan the front page (3 min):</strong> identify the 4–6 genuinely exam-relevant stories.</li>
<li><strong>Read editorials &amp; op-eds (15–18 min):</strong> for each, note the issue, key arguments both ways, and any data/reports.</li>
<li><strong>Cover governance/economy/environment/IR (15 min):</strong> read the issue, not every line.</li>
<li><strong>Note-make as you go (8–10 min):</strong> add to your single topic-wise note in bullets — don't write essays.</li>
</ol>
<p>That's a complete, high-yield read in under an hour, every day.</p>

<h2>Building the One Note That Works</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Organise by theme, not date.</strong> Sections like Polity, Economy, Environment, IR, S&amp;T, Ethics, Schemes — so at revision you see a subject's whole story together.</li>
<li><strong>Bullets, not paragraphs.</strong> Each entry: the issue + 2–3 arguments + a fact/data point.</li>
<li><strong>Keep it evolving.</strong> Append new developments under the same theme rather than starting fresh.</li>
<li><strong>Revise monthly.</strong> A note you never revisit is a diary, not a study tool.</li>
<li><strong>Tag Prelims vs Mains relevance</strong> lightly, so revision is targeted near each exam.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Newspaper + Primary Sources = Complete Current Affairs</h2>
<p>The newspaper gives you understanding; primary sources give you accuracy. Pair your daily read with:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://pib.gov.in" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">PIB</a> for official scheme details and government announcements.</li>
<li>The <a href="https://www.indiabudget.gov.in/economicsurvey/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Economic Survey</a> and <a href="https://www.indiabudget.gov.in" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Union Budget</a> for economy data.</li>
<li>Official newspaper sites — <a href="https://www.thehindu.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">The Hindu</a> and <a href="https://indianexpress.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">The Indian Express</a> (the Express "Explained" section is especially beginner-friendly).</li>
</ul>

<h2>The Hindu vs Indian Express — Which One?</h2>
<table>
<thead><tr><th>Paper</th><th>Strengths</th><th>Best for</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>The Hindu</td><td>Depth on national affairs, editorials, IR</td><td>Serious editorial analysis</td></tr>
<tr><td>Indian Express</td><td>"Explained" section, issue-based, readable</td><td>Beginners &amp; quick issue clarity</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<p><strong>Pick one and stay consistent.</strong> The "best" newspaper is the one you read fully every day. Don't split attention across two.</p>

<h2>Newspaper Mistakes That Waste Your Year</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Reading cover to cover</strong> for hours — the classic beginner trap.</li>
<li><strong>Highlighting everything,</strong> extracting nothing.</li>
<li><strong>Reading two or three papers</strong> daily and finishing none well.</li>
<li><strong>Date-wise notes</strong> you never revise (make theme-wise instead).</li>
<li><strong>Memorising events</strong> instead of understanding issues.</li>
<li><strong>Starting late</strong> — current affairs is a compounding habit; begin in month one. See <a href="/resources/upsc-beginners-guide">the beginner's guide</a>.</li>
</ol>

<h2>Your Newspaper Checklist</h2>
<ul>
<li>☐ I read one newspaper daily in a fixed 45–60 minute window.</li>
<li>☐ I read for issues, not events.</li>
<li>☐ I prioritise editorials, governance, economy, environment, IR.</li>
<li>☐ I skip celebrity/sport/crime without guilt.</li>
<li>☐ I maintain one theme-wise note in bullets.</li>
<li>☐ I revise the note monthly and pair it with PIB.</li>
</ul>
