<BODY>
<p>Every beginner's first instinct is to build a colour-coded 14-hour timetable. Two weeks later it's abandoned, and guilt replaces it. Here's the mentor truth: <strong>the best UPSC timetable isn't the most ambitious one — it's the one you can repeat tomorrow, and the day after, for a year.</strong></p>
<p>This is a realistic, phase-wise study plan built for actual human beings — full-time aspirants, working professionals and college students alike. You'll get a 12-month macro roadmap, daily timetables you can copy, weekly targets, and a revision system that makes what you study actually stick. No heroics, no screenshots for Instagram — just a plan that compounds.</p>
<blockquote><p><strong>Start here:</strong> if you haven't yet, read the <a href="/resources/upsc-beginners-guide">Day-1 beginner's guide</a> and understand the <a href="/resources/upsc-exam-pattern-syllabus">exam pattern &amp; syllabus</a> first — a plan only works once you know what you're planning for.</p></blockquote>

<h2>Key Takeaways (Read This First)</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Plan in three layers:</strong> macro (year) → weekly (chapters + answers) → daily (fixed slots).</li>
<li><strong>6–8 focused hours</strong> for full-timers; <strong>3–4</strong> for working/college aspirants. Consistency &gt; intensity.</li>
<li><strong>Current affairs + answer writing start in month one</strong> — never "after the syllabus."</li>
<li><strong>20–30% of time is revision.</strong> Daily recall, weekly consolidation, monthly review.</li>
<li><strong>Fix the non-negotiables first</strong> (sleep, one deep-work block, newspaper), then fill the rest.</li>
<li><strong>Review weekly and adjust.</strong> A plan is a hypothesis, not a contract.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The 3-Layer Planning System</h2>
<p>Most timetables fail because they jump straight to hourly slots without a bigger structure. Build top-down:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Macro plan (the year):</strong> which subjects in which months, so you finish the foundation with time to revise.</li>
<li><strong>Weekly plan (the engine):</strong> specific chapters, number of answers to write, current-affairs consolidation. This is where real accountability lives.</li>
<li><strong>Daily timetable (the habit):</strong> fixed slots for deep work, newspaper and revision so you don't decide every morning what to do.</li>
</ol>

<p>This is a template — compress or stretch it to your timeline, but keep the sequence. It maps to the same journey stages we use with foundation students.</p>

<blockquote><p><strong>Mentor note:</strong> the early-morning block is the single highest-leverage habit for working aspirants. Willpower and time are both freshest before the day's demands arrive. Protect it ruthlessly.</p></blockquote>

<h2>Your Weekly Template (The Real Accountability Layer)</h2>
<p>A day can go wrong; a week shouldn't. Plan every Sunday for the week ahead:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Static targets:</strong> e.g., "Laxmikanth Ch. 1–6" or "Leong climatology."</li>
<li><strong>Optional target:</strong> a fixed weekly chunk from month 4 onward.</li>
<li><strong>Answer writing:</strong> 5–7 answers this week (one per study day), each evaluated.</li>
<li><strong>Current affairs:</strong> consolidate the week's notes into your monthly file.</li>
<li><strong>One weekly test</strong> (Prelims MCQs or a Mains answer set) from Phase 2.</li>
<li><strong>Sunday review:</strong> what got done, what slipped, adjust next week.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The Revision System (Where Marks Are Actually Made)</h2>
<p>Aspirants who "finish the syllabus" but don't revise walk into the exam and blank. Build revision into the timetable, not around it:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Daily recall (30 min):</strong> close the book, recall the day's key points, then check gaps.</li>
<li><strong>Weekly consolidation:</strong> a fast pass over the week's notes and one set of PYQs.</li>
<li><strong>Monthly review:</strong> revise current-affairs notes and one full static subject.</li>
<li><strong>Pre-exam multiple revisions:</strong> your limited sources, read 3–4 more times.</li>
</ol>
<p>This is why "limited sources" matters so much — you can only revise repeatedly what you kept small. See the <a href="/resources/best-books-for-upsc">booklist</a> and <a href="/resources/how-to-read-ncerts-for-upsc">NCERT method</a>.</p>

<h2>How to Actually Stick to Your Timetable</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Plan the week, not just the day</strong> — daily plans break; weekly plans absorb bad days.</li>
<li><strong>Fix non-negotiables first:</strong> 7 hours sleep, one deep-work block, the newspaper. Build the rest around them.</li>
<li><strong>Use time-blocking, not to-do lists</strong> — assign hours to tasks, not tasks to a vague "later."</li>
<li><strong>Track output, not hours</strong> — "wrote 5 answers, revised Polity" beats "studied 8 hours."</li>
<li><strong>Build in one lighter day</strong> to prevent burnout; sustainability wins the marathon.</li>
<li><strong>Review and adjust weekly</strong> — treat your plan as a living hypothesis.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Common Timetable Mistakes (Avoid These)</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>The 14-hour fantasy plan</strong> that collapses in a week. Start with what you can sustain.</li>
<li><strong>Copying a topper's routine</strong> — your hours, strengths and life are different. See <a href="/resources/upsc-common-mistakes-beginners">common beginner mistakes</a>.</li>
<li><strong>No revision slots</strong> — reading once and moving on.</li>
<li><strong>Delaying answer writing</strong> until "after the syllabus."</li>
<li><strong>All input, no output</strong> — endless reading with no tests or answers to measure progress.</li>
</ol>
